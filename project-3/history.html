A CONTROVERSIAL HISTORY

This story, though just an example, is not unique. The history of Guantánamo Bay is filled with controversy and strife, from the treatment of migrants found at sea, to the false capture of men in the Middle East and the inhumane and horrific treatment they faced in Guantánamo Bay. Multiple sources have pointed to bounties given by the U.S. militaries to poorer villages in Pakistan and Afghanistan promising large sums of money for turning over "murderers." Many of the men sent to Guantánamo Bay were regular citizens, at times even refugees.

"Get wealth and power beyond your dreams . . . You can receive millions of dollars helping the anti-Taliban forces catch al-Qaida and Taliban murderers. This is enough money to take care of your family, your village, your tribe for the rest of your life. Pay for livestock and doctors and school books and housing for all your people." ("CITY ON THE HILL OR PRISON ON THE BAY? THE MISTAKES OF GUANTANAMO AND THE DECLINE OF AMERICA’S IMAGE" Hearing before the House of Representatives, page 49)

Guantánamo Bay became synonymous with covert and cruel American operations towards "enemies of the state," who received little legal counsel and disappeared without warning for years. Certain men were eventually returned home, demonstrating that the unlawful detainment they faced did not necesarily prove their guilt. Many men recounted feeling coerced to give false confessions just to get a reprieve from the torture (CNN article). Perhaps most pertinent today are the legal rights (or lack thereof) of the prisoners of Guantánamo Bay.

Recently in 2020, a federal appeals court ruled that detainees were not given due process, with an indefinite detainee's only constitutional right being to challenge his detention (New York Times). Although a larger court granted a rehearing, the issue remains contested and unsolved.
