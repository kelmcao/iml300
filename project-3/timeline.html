1903

Old photograph of Guantánamo Bay taken from Vanderbilt Prof. Paul Kramer's website.

The United States obtains a perpetual lease on Guantánamo Bay after the Spanish–American War, creating a long-term military foothold in Cuba. The base later becomes a strategic and legal gray zone for detention practices. Its isolated location and ambiguous sovereignty enable U.S. authorities to operate outside normal judicial oversight. This sets the stage for future human-rights controversies.

1970s–2001

Photo of Haitian refugees protesting at Guantánamo Bay in 1993. Taken from Guantánamo Public Memory Project.

Before it held terrorism suspects, the base was used to detain Haitian and Cuban migrants interdicted at sea. Many sought asylum, but conditions were harsh, medical care was limited, and legal access was restricted. Detainees were often held in overcrowded camps and pressured or processed for repatriation rather than asylum. These early practices foreshadowed the human-rights issues that would define Guantánamo.

2002

Photo of prisoners at Guantánamo Bay taken in 2002 depicting harsh conditions, from "The New York Times".

The U.S. begins holding suspected terrorists at Guantánamo as “enemy combatants,” outside traditional criminal law protections. Reports of torture, forced interrogation, and indefinite detention without trial quickly emerge. The facility becomes globally infamous as a symbol of human-rights violations. Its legal isolation allows the U.S. government to avoid due-process requirements applied on U.S. soil.

2004–2008

Photo of protestors at the Supreme Court on the 5th anniversary of the prison's opening, 2007. Taken from investigative journalist and historian Andy Worthington's website.

Between 2004 and 2008, a series of landmark Supreme Court cases reshaped Guantánamo’s legal landscape. In Rasul v. Bush (2004), the Court ruled that detainees could challenge their detention in U.S. federal courts, rejecting the government’s claim that Guantánamo lay outside judicial reach. The Bush administration responded by creating military tribunals, but the Court struck them down in Hamdan v. Rumsfeld (2006), declaring the system unconstitutional and in violation of the Geneva Conventions. Congress then passed the Military Commissions Act (2006) to limit detainees’ rights, but the Court again pushed back in Boumediene v. Bush (2008), affirming detainees’ constitutional right to habeas corpus and exposing systemic abuses and legal evasions.

2009–2016

Photo of Obama signing a directive to shut down Guantánamo Bay in 2009. Found in NPR article released in 2013.

The Obama administration attempts to close the facility and transfers many detainees, reducing the population significantly. However, political resistance blocks full closure, leaving the infrastructure and legal framework intact. Human-rights organizations warn that the persistence of Guantánamo normalizes indefinite detention. The site remains a global emblem of extrajudicial imprisonment.

2025

Newly erected tents for holding migrants in Guantánamo Bay, as found in PBS article from March covering lawyers' fight to sue Trump for the illegal and inhumane detainment of immigrants in Guantánamo.

For the first time in its history, Guantánamo is designated to receive large numbers of migrants apprehended from inside the U.S., not just those intercepted at sea. The administration expands the Migrant Operations Center to handle up to tens of thousands of people. Rights groups criticize the move as reviving Guantánamo’s legacy of secrecy and abuse—now applied to immigrants with no criminal charges. Advocates warn that detained migrants face limited legal access, potential repatriation pressures, and conditions reminiscent of earlier, discredited practices.
